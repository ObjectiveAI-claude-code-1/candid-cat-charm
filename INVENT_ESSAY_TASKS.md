Task 1 — Evaluate Authenticity of Behavior: Assess whether the cat in the image is engaged in genuine, natural behavior that arises from its own instincts and impulses, rather than being posed, costumed, propped up, or otherwise arranged for the camera. Look for the hallmarks of a cat being itself — kneading, leaping, sleeping in absurd positions, stalking, startling, grooming, or simply existing in that particular way cats do — and distinguish these from images where the cat has been turned into a prop or a vehicle for human cleverness. A cat expressing its own nature should score highly. A cat whose behavior has been staged, forced, or contrived should score low. The key question is: does this cat's behavior feel real, the kind of thing a cat would do whether or not anyone was watching?

Task 2 — Evaluate Emotional Resonance of the Moment: Assess whether the image produces a genuine emotional response — delight, tenderness, laughter, warmth — that arises naturally and inseparably from the moment itself, rather than from artificial packaging layered on top of it. Consider whether the image would still move you if you stripped away any captions, filters, overlaid text, or framing devices. An image where the feeling lives in the moment — the curl of a sleeping cat's paw, the wide-eyed alarm of a startled kitten, the gentle press of a cat leaning into a human hand — should score highly. An image that relies on external embellishments to manufacture an emotional reaction it does not inherently possess should score low. The key question is: does the emotional power of this picture come from what is actually happening in it, or from how it has been dressed up?

Task 3 — Evaluate the Quality of Witnessing: Assess whether the image feels like a moment that was discovered and caught rather than deliberately produced. Look for the texture of accident, spontaneity, and fortunate timing — the signs that someone was simply present, paying attention, and recognized something worth preserving. Imperfect framing, natural lighting, the ordinary clutter of a real environment, and the sense of a fleeting instant frozen in time all contribute to this quality. A photo that feels like a small act of witnessing — someone sharing something they were lucky enough to see — should score highly. A photo that announces its own production through deliberate staging, controlled backdrops, professional lighting setups, or the unmistakable air of a photoshoot should score low. The key question is: does this image feel like it was found or made?
