Task 1 — Evaluate Behavioral Authenticity: Examine the cat in the image and determine whether it is engaged in genuine, unperformed behavior. Look at the cat's body language, posture, and physical state for signs of real feline behavior — the boneless limpness of true sleep, the committed wildness of a mid-leap body, the half-closed eyes of contented kneading, the alert crouch before a pounce. Assess whether the moment feels like something the cat arrived at on its own, or whether it appears to have been arranged by human hands. Check for signs of staging or contrivance: costumes, constructed sets, treats visibly luring the cat's gaze toward the camera, or poses that feel physically unnatural for a cat to adopt voluntarily. Note that human presence does not automatically reduce authenticity — a cat pressing into a person's hand or curled in someone's lap can be deeply authentic — but the interaction must feel mutual and genuine rather than forced. Score higher when the cat's behavior is unmistakably real, and lower when the moment feels manufactured or performed.

Task 2 — Evaluate Emotional Resonance: Assess whether the captured moment carries emotional weight — whether it reveals something about the inner life or character of the cat that stirs a genuine feeling in the viewer. Look for moments of vulnerability made visible: a kitten trusting the world enough to sleep in an exposed position, a cat surrendering to affection with closed eyes, an elderly cat resting in quiet contentment. Look equally for moments of feline comedy and absurdity: a cat wedged into a container far too small for its body, the intense focus of a cat stalking something trivial, the dignified outrage of a cat that has been mildly inconvenienced. Recognize that charm operates across many emotional registers — warmth, humor, tenderness, surprise — and that what unites them is the feeling that we are seeing something true about who this cat is. Score higher when the image evokes a sense of tender or delighted witnessing, and lower when the moment, however authentic, feels flat or unremarkable — a cat simply existing without revealing anything that invites an emotional response.

Task 3 — Evaluate Spontaneous Aliveness: Determine whether the photograph itself feels like a seized moment — a small act of serendipity that preserved something fleeting — rather than a carefully constructed composition. Look for the hallmarks of spontaneity: motion blur that suggests a moment too fast to be perfectly frozen, imperfect framing where the cat is partially out of the shot because the photographer was reacting rather than composing, natural and uncontrolled lighting, and backgrounds that show the ordinary texture of real life — rumpled blankets, cluttered shelves, the lived-in evidence of a home shared with a cat. Conversely, note when an image feels overproduced: studio-quality lighting, meticulous composition, artificially clean or curated settings that suggest the scene was designed rather than discovered. The goal is to reward images that carry a sense of temporal urgency — the feeling that this moment almost didn't get captured, that the shutter clicked just in time. Score higher when the photograph feels like a fragment rescued from the flow of ordinary life, and lower when it feels like a planned production, regardless of how technically polished the result may be.
